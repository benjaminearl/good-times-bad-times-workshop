→ Introduction

Radio is on the rise again. Especially since COVID-19 hit and alternative ways of interacting and communicating were needed, new online radio initiatives seem to be popping up everywhere. Some may say it’s just another quarantine hype, nevertheless the medium has great potential as an educational tool.

For the past years there have been radio stations and collectives broadcasting from various art schools and institutions around The Netherlands, such as Ja Ja Ja Nee Nee Nee, Radio Rietveld, Sandberg’s PUB, WT’s Phantom Radio and KABK’s Mushroom Radio – to name just a few. The internet radio ‘scene’ is very non-competitive community-oriented, where stations are happy to share knowledge and plug each other. There is even talk about using radio in unionising students from art schools nation-wide. It’s a medium that seems to empower and bring people together.

Besides a platform for sonic experimentation or showcasing anything audio-based, radio provides a concentrated space for discussion and reflection, due to its purely auditory nature. Focusing on language through one sense only, thoughts unfold linearly on both the producing and receiving end of the process. This contributes to the appeal of radio as opposed to other types of media where we are bombarded with visual prompts that distract from the message.

Still the visual side of online radio should not be overlooked. Before you’ve got your audience tuning in, they need to know where and when to hit that play button. In this workshop we’ll run through all stages of developing a radio station, from learning about hardware and software, creating a web server and website to host your station to experimenting with the interplay of audio content and its visual vessel.
